What is phishing?

A social engineering attack that tricks you into revealing sensitive information.

High risk

The goal of a phishing attack

Attackers impersonate trusted entities — banks, employers, services like Google or PayPal — to steal credentials, install malware, or gain unauthorized access to systems.

Common targets

Who gets targeted?

Everyone. But attackers specifically target employees with system access, people who handle finances, and anyone whose credentials unlock valuable data.

Real impact

By the numbers

Over 90% of cyberattacks begin with a phishing email. It's the most common entry point for ransomware, data breaches, and corporate espionage.

Spot the phishing email

Study this fake email and learn to recognize the red flags.

From:security-alerts@paypa1.support-team.com
To:you@gmail.com
Subject:URGENT: Your account has been suspended

Dear Valued Customer,


We have detected suspicious activity on your PayPal account. Your account has been temporarily suspended for your protection.


You must verify your information within 24 hours or your account will be permanently closed.


Click here to verify your account immediately


PayPal Security Team

Red flags in this email

Fake domain: "paypa1.support-team.com" — not paypal.com. The "l" is replaced with "1".
Urgency tactic: "24 hours or permanently closed" — designed to panic you into acting fast.
Vague greeting: "Dear Valued Customer" — real companies use your name.
Suspicious link: Never click links in unexpected emails. Go directly to the website instead.
Threatening language: Legitimate companies don't threaten to close accounts over email without warning.

Social engineering tactics

Attackers exploit human psychology, not just technology.

Tactic 1

Urgency & fear

"Your account will be deleted in 24 hours." Panic kills rational thinking. Attackers create fake deadlines to stop you from verifying the request.

Tactic 2

Authority impersonation

Pretending to be your CEO, IT department, bank, or government. People comply with authority figures without questioning.

Tactic 3

Spear phishing

Highly targeted attacks using your name, employer, and personal details gathered from LinkedIn or social media to appear legitimate.

Tactic 4

Smishing & vishing

Phishing via SMS (smishing) or voice calls (vishing). "This is Amazon, your package is on hold — press 1 to verify."

Tactic 5

Pretexting

Creating a believable story to extract information. "Hi, I'm from IT support, I need your credentials to fix a server issue."

Best practices

Simple habits that stop most phishing attacks.

Verify the URL

Hover over links before clicking. Check for misspellings like paypa1.com or g00gle.com.

Enable 2FA

Two-factor authentication stops attackers even if they steal your password.

Question urgency

Any message creating panic or a deadline is a red flag. Slow down and verify.

Call to verify

If an email asks for credentials or money, call the person directly using a known number.

Don't open attachments

Unexpected .pdf, .zip, or .docx files can contain malware. Verify before opening.

Keep software updated

Patches fix vulnerabilities that phishing malware exploits. Update regularly.
